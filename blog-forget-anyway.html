"They'll Forget It Anyway": How Parents Use a Child's Age to Avoid Accountability

"They're too young to remember" is the most convenient lie in parenting. A free pass. A get-out-of-accountability card adults play on themselves whenever they want to lose control without paying for it later. This is for the parent who needs to put the mirror up, and the young person who's been told for years their pain isn't real.

The Myth of the Blank Slate

"Don't worry, they're too young, they won't even remember this."

It's the line that ends almost every honest conversation about how an adult just spoke to a small child. A free pass. A get-out-of-accountability card the grown-up plays on themselves whenever they want to lose control without paying for it later.

It's not science. It's psychological cowardice.

A three-year-old won't remember the words of an argument. They won't be able to recite the exact insult, recall what the shouting was about, or summarise the night for a therapist twenty years later. But their nervous system will. Their body will. The cortisol that spiked when their parent screamed will leave a chemical fingerprint that gets handed forward into every relationship, every job interview, every panic attack, every Sunday-night dread for the rest of their life.

The blank slate is a myth. The body keeps the receipts.

This piece is written for two people at once — the parent who needs to put the mirror up, and the young person who has spent years being told their pain isn't real. Both of you matter. Both of you need to hear different parts of the same truth.

1. The Bio-Data Trap — The Body Remembers Everything

Most adults think memory works like a hard drive: events get saved as words and pictures, and if you weren't old enough to encode the file, it doesn't exist. Convenient theory.

It's wrong.

Memory in early childhood is somatic. It's stored in the nervous system, in muscle tension, in the way the body braces, in the speed of the breath, in the cortisol baseline that gets set before a child can even speak in sentences. The "memory" of an unsafe home isn't a story the brain replays — it's a stance the body takes, every day, for decades.

The Mirror — for the parent

When you scream. When you slam a door. When you punish with the cold shoulder for three days because your kid said something you didn't like. When you freeze them out until they apologise for something that wasn't their fault.

You are not just having a bad moment. You are programming a small nervous system. You are setting its baseline.

That baseline is called hyper-vigilance. It is what we used to mean by "shell shock." It is the body learning that the people who are meant to be safe are not, and that survival depends on watching them constantly. And then — fourteen years later — you sit across from a quiet teenager and ask why they're so anxious, why they can't focus, why they "have ADHD now," why they flinch when you raise your voice.

You did that. Not entirely. Not on purpose. But materially, neurologically: you did that.

The Voice — for the young person

If your chest tightens every time a door slams. If your stomach drops when someone's tone of voice changes. If you can read the temperature of a room before anyone has spoken.

You are not "too sensitive." You are not "overreacting." You are not "making a big deal out of nothing."

Your body is doing exactly what it was trained to do. It learnt, very young, that reading the danger early was the only way to stay safe. That isn't a flaw. That's a survival skill that worked. The problem isn't you — it's that the danger is supposed to be over, and your body hasn't been told yet.

You are allowed to grieve the version of you that never got to relax.

2. The Cowardice of the Easy Target

Let's get honest about power.

Adults who lose their tempers at their children almost never lose their tempers at their boss, their landlord, or their friend group with the same intensity. They don't scream at the colleague who messed up the report. They don't slam doors at the partner who bought the wrong pasta. They don't withhold love from the friend who shows up late.

Why?

Because those people can leave. Those people can fight back. Those people have power.

A child has none.

The Mirror — for the parent

The next time the rage rises at your kid, ask yourself one question: would I be speaking to anyone else in the world like this?

If the answer is no, then what you are doing isn't discipline. It isn't "tough love." It isn't "they need to hear it." It's bullying. It is a release valve. You are using the one person in your house who can't fire you, can't leave you, can't argue back with the resources of an adult, as a containment vessel for stress that has nothing to do with them.

You are emotionally bullying a child because your boss humiliated you, your bank account is empty, your marriage is brittle, or your own mother's voice is still in your head and you would rather hand it down than face it.

That is not parenting. That is emotional bullying with a parental licence.

The Voice — for the young person

You are not the problem.

You were the available problem.

When an adult chooses to lose it at a child instead of going for a walk, calling a friend, working out, going to therapy, or simply shutting their mouth — that is a choice they made. You happened to be in the room. You did not trigger it. You did not deserve it. You were not "winding them up."

You were the easiest target in the house for stress that didn't start with you.

You are allowed to know that, even if you still love them.

3. The "I Had It Worse" Cop-Out

The generational trauma pyramid scheme works like this: my parents hit me, I only shout at you, my grandparents shouted at my parents, so really we're improving.

It's the most popular escape hatch in the history of family dysfunction. And it doesn't work.

The Mirror — for the parent

"My dad used to belt me with the back of his hand, and I turned out fine."

You did not turn out fine.

You are standing in the kitchen shouting at a nine-year-old over a cup. You are three drinks deep on a Tuesday because that is the only way you can come down. You haven't slept properly since you were six. You can't say "I love you" without it sounding like a warning. You are using a smaller cruelty than your parents used and calling that healing.

That is not healing. That is a downgrade in the dosage of the same poison.

"Better than my parents" is not the bar. The bar is: my child does not flinch when I walk into the room. If you can't meet that bar, you are not done. You are mid-cycle. And the cycle doesn't end by accident.

The Voice — for the young person

You will be told, over and over, that your pain doesn't count because somebody else had it worse.

Your mum had it worse. Your dad had it worse. Children in war zones have it worse. The neighbour's kid has it worse. There is always someone you can be measured against to make your suffering small.

This is not how pain works.

Trauma is not a competition. There is no league table. The fact that your parent grew up in something darker does not cancel what they did in the light. You do not owe an abuser an extra pass because their abuser was scarier.

Your pain is valid in its own currency. You do not have to translate it.

4. The Price of Delayed Healing

There is a bill at the end of this.

It is called estrangement.

The Mirror — for the parent

Right now, your child is small. You feed them. You house them. You decide when the lights go off. You can ignore the look on their face. You can dismiss what they tell you. You can interrupt their cry and call it whining. You can tell them they're being dramatic. You can be wrong and still win, because they have nowhere to go.

That window closes.

It closes at eighteen if they get a job. Earlier if a friend's parent steps in. At twenty-five when their salary clears their rent. At thirty when their therapist asks them to write the letter. But it closes.

On the other side of that window, there's a quiet phone. There is a Christmas you weren't invited to. There is a grandchild you've never met. There is a future where someone you raised has decided that the most loving thing they can do for themselves — and for their own family — is to keep you out of it.

Estrangement is not ingratitude. Estrangement is a delayed invoice.

You can pay it now, while they still want you in their life. Or you can keep refusing, and they will pay it for both of you, by leaving.

The Voice — for the young person

You do not owe a relationship to someone who refuses to see the damage they have caused.

Not because they fed you. Not because they housed you. Not because they "did their best with what they had." Those are the minimum requirements of being a parent. Meeting the minimum does not entitle anyone to access to your adult life.

If, when you're old enough to make the choice, you decide to step back — low contact, no contact, grey-rock, quiet distance — that is not a failure of love. It is an act of self-respect that the people who raised you should have modelled for you and didn't.

You are allowed to choose peace.

"Children do not learn from what you tell them; they learn from how you make them feel. If your home feels like a battlefield, they will grow up looking for wars to fight."

The Call to Action

For the parent

You don't get points for putting a roof over a child's head. That is the floor, not the ceiling. Real love is a regulated nervous system in the next room. It is a steady voice. It is an apology, said out loud, to a small person who needed to hear it.

Apologising to your child — even your toddler — does not undermine your authority. It teaches them what accountability looks like. It teaches them that adults are allowed to be wrong, and that being wrong does not end love. They will carry that into every relationship they ever have.

Start now. Today. With one sentence: "I shouldn't have spoken to you like that. That was about my stuff, not yours, and I'm sorry."

That sentence is the beginning. The rest is practice.

For the young person

You are the one who notices. That makes you the cycle-breaker.

Knowing that something is wrong is not the same as fixing it. But it is the first thing. You can name what happened. You can refuse to inherit it. You can decide that the way you were spoken to is not the way you will speak to anyone — to your friends, to your partner, to a future child, to yourself.

The fact that you are reading this means part of you already knows. Trust that part.

You are not broken. You are awake.

If you need to talk to someone now. Childline: 0800 1111 (free, 24/7, confidential). Shout: text SHOUT to 85258. Samaritans: 116 123. NSPCC adult helpline (if you're worried about a child): 0808 800 5000. None of these will judge you. None of them will report you unless someone is in immediate danger.

You're not the only one who noticed.
