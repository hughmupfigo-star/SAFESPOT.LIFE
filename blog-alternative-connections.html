Alternative Connections: Where Your Real People Are Hiding

For the disabled, LGBTQ+, neurodivergent or isolated young person, social media wasn't entertainment — it was the only place you'd found people like you. Under the under-16 ban, here are the spaces that are still legal, still safe, and still worth your time. Plus what to ask for in the world off the screen.

If you are reading this, the under-16 ban means you have just lost something. Not "screen time". Something more specific. The group chat you spent two years building. The creators who taught you the words for what you were feeling. The fifteen people across three countries who know what you actually look like with the mask off.

For most people, social media was entertainment. For some people, it was the only place they had ever found their own kind. Adults who never needed it to find each other are about to make decisions about you that assume you did not need it either. They are wrong.

So before we do anything else: what you lost was real. You are allowed to be angry about it. You are allowed to grieve it. You are also allowed to start, today, finding the next version of it — and that next version, with a little curation, can be safer, slower, and more yours.

This piece is a directory. The first half covers what is still legal under the ban. The second half is the in-person stuff. Skim, scan, save, share.

What's still legal under the ban (and what isn't)

The under-16 ban targets algorithmic social media platforms — products whose primary business model is ranking and recommending content to keep you scrolling. That definition matters, because it leaves a lot of internet untouched. Broadly:

  • Still legal: Messaging apps (WhatsApp, Signal, iMessage). Educational platforms. Heavily moderated forums and online clubs. Specific-interest sites (chess, code, books, music, art). Games with chat. Email. Most of the actually useful internet.
  • Not legal under 16: TikTok, Instagram (the main feeds), Snapchat, YouTube's main recommendation feed, X, Threads, BeReal, and anything with an algorithmic "For You" page as the central product.

The point of this directory is to show you that "no social media" does not mean "no community". It means picking your spaces more carefully than the algorithm picked them for you.

For peer support that's safer than what you lost

Childline Message Boards

Under 19 · UK · NSPCC-moderated · Free

Heavily moderated online boards run by Childline (NSPCC). You can post anonymously about anything that is going on. Real moderators read posts. Other young people reply. Lower volume than Instagram, far higher quality of conversation.

childline.org.uk/message-boards →

The Mix

Under 25 · UK · Moderated forums + 1-to-1 chat · Free

UK youth charity with moderated discussion forums, live one-to-one webchat, and a phone line (0808 808 4994). Discussions on mental health, money, drugs, relationships, sexuality, housing. Designed for the under-25 audience and treated as part of the educational/support framework.

themix.org.uk →

YoungMinds Activists Community

11–25 · UK · Moderated · Mental health · Free

YoungMinds runs moderated peer spaces specifically for young people wanting to talk about mental health, share experience and campaign together. Email-based and event-based, not algorithmic.

youngminds.org.uk →

For LGBTQ+ young people specifically

TrevorSpace

13–24 · International · LGBTQ+ · Moderated · Free

The largest moderated international peer community for LGBTQ+ young people. Run by The Trevor Project. Trained moderators. Forums, friendships, regional groups. It is a recognised support service, so it should remain accessible under the moderated-community exemption.

trevorspace.org →

Mermaids UK

Under 20 · UK · Trans & gender-diverse youth · Moderated · Free

UK charity for transgender, non-binary and gender-diverse young people and their families. Helpline, web chat, parent groups, regional youth groups. Designed exactly for the young people most vulnerable to home isolation.

mermaidsuk.org.uk →

Switchboard LGBTQIA+ Helpline

All ages · UK · Free · 10am–10pm daily

A real human, on a phone, instant message or email, every day from 10am to 10pm. Forty years of practice. Not a forum but worth knowing about — the person on the other end is gay, lesbian, bi, trans or non-binary themselves.

switchboard.lgbt →

For neurodivergent and disabled young people

National Autistic Society — Young People's Community

UK · Autistic young people · Moderated · Free

Forums, events, advice and resources specifically by and for autistic young people. Designed for the bandwidth and sensory preferences of the audience. Forums are slow, moderated, accessible.

autism.org.uk →

Scope Online Community

UK · Disabled young people and families · Moderated · Free

Run by Scope (UK disability charity). Moderated forums by topic. Other disabled young people who actually understand the day-to-day. Not algorithmic, not addictive, not commercial.

community.scope.org.uk →

For finding people through a shared interest

One of the cleanest ways to build a real community online is around a thing you both already care about. These sites are not "social media" — they are platforms with chat built in around a clear shared activity.

  • chess.com and Lichess — global chess communities. Both have moderated club functions and an enormous youth scene.
  • GitHub — if you are learning to code. Open-source projects often have welcoming under-18 contributors.
  • Scratch (scratch.mit.edu) — the original creative-coding community for younger teens. Heavily moderated, designed for safety.
  • Goodreads and StoryGraph — book communities, reading challenges, slow-paced conversations about what you are reading.
  • BandLab and Splice — collaborative music platforms where the conversation is about your actual track.
  • Letterboxd — film community. Reviews, lists, watchlists. Genuinely warm.
  • Wattpad — writing community with strict content moderation for under-18 accounts.

Most of these will continue to be legal under any sensible reading of the ban because the algorithmic feed is not the product. The product is the chess game, the code, the song, the book.

For the boring brilliant thing nobody mentions: messaging

WhatsApp, Signal, iMessage, Telegram, Discord. None of these are "social media" in the regulatory sense. They are messaging. They will not be banned for under-16s.

Two suggestions, if this is going to become a bigger part of your life:

  • Build small group chats around real friendships. Six to twelve people who actually know each other. Not the giant year-group chat where bullying lives. The small one, with the people you would tell something to.
  • For Discord, choose servers carefully. Look for servers with verified moderators, slow-mode chat, age-gated channels, and rules that are actually enforced. Avoid anything that is essentially anonymous and chaotic. The good Discord servers are like the best version of a community centre. The bad ones are the worst version of an unmoderated forum.

For when the internet is not the answer

This bit is genuinely hard if you are isolated. But it is also the bit that does the most for your nervous system over time.

  • Your local library. Every UK library runs free clubs — reading, coding, board games, knitting, writing, chess, language exchange. Walk in. Ask at the desk. It costs nothing.
  • Volunteering. Free, places you immediately around people. Do-it.org and volunteer.scot have age-appropriate options from 14 upward.
  • Scouts, Guides, ATC, Sea Cadets, Army Cadets, Boys' Brigade. Heavily subsidised. Skill-led. Adults are vetted. Hated by everyone who hasn't been, loved by most who have.
  • Sport at the cheap level. Parkrun (free, every Saturday morning, all ages). Council football, council swimming. Boxing gyms — most welcome teenagers and the discipline is real.
  • Faith communities, if that fits — mosques, gurdwaras, churches, temples, synagogues all run youth groups and they are often where the warmest welcomes are in towns where everything else has closed.
  • Music. Local choirs, town brass bands, community orchestras, jam sessions. If you can play one chord on one instrument, somebody wants you in their room.
  • Skateparks, climbing walls, dance studios. Find the one that's closest. Show up three times. You will know someone by visit four.

If you're a youth worker reading this

A few asks:

  • Audit what you currently signpost. The link tree on your wall probably says "follow us on Instagram". Replace it with the helplines and the moderated communities listed above.
  • Run a "Finding Your People After the Ban" session with your young people. Not to teach them — to learn from them where they are going. They will know things you do not.
  • Make space in your physical setting for the kinds of small-group conversation that used to happen in DMs. Slow rooms. No phones out for one hour. A snack. A topic. A trusted adult somewhere in the building.
  • Speak up in consultations. The young people in your service have specific evidence that the policymakers do not. Their lives are about to be used as evidence either way. Better that you help them give that evidence directly.

If you're a parent reading this

The version of you that wanted somebody to do something about the algorithm was right. The version of you that is now allowed to relax because "the law is handling it" should be careful — for a vulnerable kid in your house, the law just removed the one place they had found their people. The most loving thing you can do this month is help them find the next one.

Ask. Don't assume. Help them write into the spaces above. Drive them to the skatepark. Talk to your library. Trust that their need for community is real even when the version they had was unhealthy.

A locked door is not the same as a built room. Some of these kids needed a built room. If we cannot do that nationally, we can at least do it for the ones in our own homes.

For the young person reading this on a phone you're about to lose

Save this page. Screenshot it. Send it to one friend. Bookmark three of the places above. Write down two real-world things you might try.

You are not broken because the algorithm cared about you in the only way it knew how. You are about to find better.
