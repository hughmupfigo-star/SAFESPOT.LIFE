Beyond the Ban: The Right Diagnosis, the Wrong Prescription

Keir Starmer's under-16 social media ban is the right diagnosis with the wrong prescription. It tries to remove children from the problem rather than fix the platforms. Here are the five gaps it leaves unaddressed, what other countries are learning, and what an internet worth growing up in would actually look like.

Earlier this week the Prime Minister, Keir Starmer, announced a total ban on social media for under-16s in the UK. Algorithmic platforms — TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat, YouTube's main feed — gone for anyone under sixteen.

The reaction came in fast and in two directions at once. Parents who have been watching their thirteen-year-old's sleep, mood and attention collapse over the last three years felt the relief of someone in charge finally naming the problem. Digital rights groups, disability charities, LGBTQ+ organisations and a growing number of child psychologists were less sure. Their criticism is sharp and worth taking seriously:

It is the right diagnosis. It is the wrong prescription. The ban tries to remove children from the problem rather than fix the platforms.

That distinction matters, because if we get this wrong twice — once by leaving young people unprotected for a decade, then again by passing a law that misses what is actually causing the harm — we will be picking up the pieces for another generation.

This piece is for educators, policymakers, and parents who are tech-aware enough to want more than a headline. It is not an argument for doing nothing. It is an argument for doing the right thing instead of the easy thing.

The diagnosis is right. Let us start there.

Adolescent mental health in the UK has measurably worsened since 2012 — the year the algorithmic feed went mainstream. Probable mental disorder among 17 to 19-year-olds has roughly doubled. Self-harm presentations at A&E among teenage girls have climbed sharply. The link to social media use is not the whole story, but the data is no longer ambiguous: heavy use of algorithmic short-form video, particularly for girls, is associated with sleep loss, body image deterioration, attention fragmentation and rising rates of clinical anxiety.

Anyone who has tried to put a phone down at 1am knows that what we are calling "use" is not normal voluntary behaviour. These products are designed to be hard to stop using, by adults whose entire compensation structure rewards them for keeping our attention. The kids did not choose this fight, and they were never going to win it on their own.

So when a Prime Minister stands up and says this is a problem and we are going to act, that is not nothing. Parents who have lost arguments with their own children's home screens for years deserve to hear someone in power agree that the home screen is part of the problem.

The question is what comes next.

What the ban leaves unaddressed

Five gaps. Each one matters individually. Together they make the ban less effective than its supporters hope and more harmful than its drafters intend.

1. The "safe by design" deficit

The ban targets access. It does not touch the products. Algorithmic recommendation, infinite scroll, autoplay, hyper-personalised notification cycles, engagement-maximising ranking — none of these are changed by an age gate. The architecture that produced the harm continues to exist. It just does not let sixteens see it anymore. When the sixteenth birthday arrives, the platform is the same platform it always was.

2. The age-16 cliff edge

If you have spent five years gradually learning to drive, you are safer behind the wheel at seventeen than someone handed the keys on their birthday. The current framework gives children no graduated digital literacy. They will not be allowed near the feed at all — and then, on their sixteenth birthday, they will be dropped into the deep end of the most sophisticated attention-extraction machines ever built, without ever having been taught to swim. We do not let new drivers onto motorways. We are about to do exactly that with TikTok.

3. Cutting off lifelines for vulnerable young people

For a visually impaired teenager in a rural village, a gay fifteen-year-old in a household that would react badly, a neurodivergent kid whose only social ease is online, or a young carer who has no time for after-school clubs — social media is not entertainment. It is the only place they have ever found peers like them. Disability advocates, AKT, Mermaids, Stonewall and the National Autistic Society have all warned the same thing in different words: a blanket ban removes the only community some young people have. The intent is protection. The effect, for a subset of teenagers who were already isolated, is sentencing them to more isolation.

4. Displacement into darker spaces

When Australia rolled out its under-16 ban in early 2025, internal evidence quickly suggested that close to 70% of underage users were still on the platforms within months — via VPNs, sibling accounts, borrowed credentials, secondary devices and the long tail of platforms nobody is policing. More worrying: a measurable share migrated off moderated platforms entirely, into private Discord servers, decentralised apps, encrypted forums and pornography sites that the policy made no attempt to address. Mainstream platforms are dangerous. The places teenagers go when the mainstream is locked are usually worse — less moderation, less reporting, less ability for police, schools or parents to even know what is happening.

5. Massive privacy and age-verification risks

An effective ban means knowing — for every account, every login, every visit — exactly how old the user is. That requires some combination of biometric facial scanning, behavioural fingerprinting, ID upload, or a centralised identity register. None of these are limited in their effects to under-16s. To check whether a child is on the platform, every adult also has to prove they are not. The ban quietly creates the biggest expansion of identity verification in UK internet history, and it does so under emotional cover that makes it harder to scrutinise.

What other countries are learning

The UK is not first. Australia passed its version in November 2024, with implementation through 2025; early results suggest high circumvention, modest behavioural change, and a measurable shift of teen activity to less-moderated apps. France trialled a parental-consent regime for under-15s and quietly discovered that parents either consented automatically or could not be reached. Norway proposed a fifteen-and-over rule alongside a much stronger duty-of-care obligation on the platforms themselves — that combination, if it survives the legislative process, will probably outperform a ban on its own. Spain debated a sixteen rule and shelved it, choosing instead to fund mandatory digital-literacy education from year six.

The pattern emerging is that bans without product redesign have small effects, large unintended consequences, and high political cost. The countries getting closer to actual reductions in harm are the ones treating the platform as the regulated object — like a car, a cigarette, or a drug.

What "safe by design" would actually mean

The phrase is doing a lot of work in policy circles right now, so worth saying what it actually involves. A safe-by-design regime would require platforms to:

  • Switch off algorithmic recommendation by default for accounts under 18. Chronological feed, no autoplay, no infinite scroll. Users could opt back in at 18.
  • Cap notification delivery outside daytime hours for minor accounts. No buzz at 2am.
  • Ban dark-pattern engagement loops targeted at minors — streaks, "you've not posted in 3 days", manufactured urgency.
  • Require the platform to be able to demonstrate, with audited data, that its product is not producing measurable harm to under-18 users. Burden of proof on the platform, not the regulator.
  • Disclose recommendation logic in a form a researcher and a child psychologist can read.
  • Hard data minimisation for minor accounts. No behavioural advertising, ever.

None of this requires keeping a fourteen-year-old off the internet. It requires designing the internet differently when the user is fourteen.

What the conversation should be

The honest version of this argument is the version most platforms do not want to have. The companies making the products that are damaging children's sleep and attention have spent fifteen years and billions of pounds avoiding any regulation of the products themselves. A ban on the user is, from their perspective, the best possible outcome — it punishes the family, lets the company keep shipping the same algorithm to the same adults, and uses the political energy of worried parents as cover to keep the architecture intact.

If the under-16 ban passes without product reform attached, the platforms will be the only ones who benefit.

What young people, educators and engaged parents can ask for right now — clearly, on the record, in every consultation and every constituency surgery — is the following:

  • Pair any access restriction with binding safe-by-design obligations on the platforms.
  • Replace the cliff edge with a graduated framework — algorithmic feed off until 16, chronological-only with parental and educational support 13–16, broad access from 16 with continued data-minimisation to 18.
  • Fund mandatory digital literacy from year six. Not optional. Not bolt-on. As fundamental as reading.
  • Explicitly protect moderated peer-support spaces for disabled, LGBTQ+, neurodivergent and isolated young people. Carve them out of the ban or make sure they qualify under the educational/community exemption.
  • Reject any age-verification system that creates a national identity register or hands biometric data to private platforms.

The diagnosis was right. The question is whether we have the discipline to insist on a prescription that actually treats the disease.

An internet worth growing up in is possible. We will not get there by locking the kids out of the bad one. We get there by making the platforms account for what they have built.
