Bypassing the Gatekeepers: How to Reverse-Pitch for a Job

Stop applying to jobs on LinkedIn. Stop waiting for companies to post positions. Stop competing with 200 other applicants for a job nobody's excited about. Instead, find a business that needs what you can do, and pitch them before they even know they have an opening.

The Job Application Scam

You apply to 50 jobs. You hear back from none. Or you hear back 6 weeks later after going through automated screening designed to filter you out. Meanwhile, someone walked into a business, talked to the owner, and got hired that day.

That's not luck. That's a reverse pitch.

Here's the truth: most of the best jobs never get posted. Owners hire the person who walks in with a solution to their problem. They never have to write a job description. They never have to interview 40 people. They just hire the person who showed up and knew what was needed.

What's a Reverse Pitch?

A reverse pitch is when YOU identify what a business needs and offer to solve it—before they post a job looking for someone to solve it.

Normal pitch: "Will you give me a job?"
Reverse pitch: "I see you're struggling with X. I can fix that. Here's how. Here's what I'd charge (or: here's what I'd work for as a starting point)."

One makes you an applicant. The other makes you a problem-solver.

Step 1: Research Businesses That Exist (And Are Struggling)

You're not looking for companies hiring. You're looking for companies that are busy, disorganised, or running on outdated systems.

Signs of opportunity:

  • Their website hasn't been updated since 2018
  • Their social media is inactive or chaotic
  • They don't have clear online ordering or booking
  • They seem to have high turnover (always hiring)
  • They're growing fast and likely overwhelmed
  • They're a local business (café, salon, shop, agency, startup)

Where to find them:

  • Walk around your local area. Actually look at businesses.
  • Google "[your area] + [type of business]"
  • LinkedIn: local businesses often have outdated profiles
  • Instagram: look for businesses with poor posting consistency or chaotic pages
  • Local job boards: see who's constantly hiring (sign of chaos)

The goal: find 15-20 businesses that could probably use help.

Step 2: Identify Their Problem (The Crucial Part)

Visit their business. Look around. Talk to staff. What's broken? What's annoying them?

Common problems you can solve:

  • Social media chaos: Posts are inconsistent, comments go unanswered, nobody knows what their brand is
  • Booking/ordering nightmare: Customers email or call instead of booking online. Phone's always ringing.
  • Data chaos: Customer information is scattered. No system for tracking repeat customers.
  • Email overload: Tons of customer emails going to different places. Responses are slow.
  • Admin burden: The owner/manager is doing 10 jobs and drowning.
  • Website looks terrible: No mobile site. Outdated design. Nobody can find basic info.
  • Accounting mess: Invoices are scattered. Income tracking is manual.

Pick one thing. The thing that's obviously costing them money or time.

Step 3: Create a Proposal (Keep It Simple)

This isn't a formal business proposal. It's a one-page document that says:

"I noticed you're struggling with X. Here's how that's costing you. Here's what I can do about it. Here's how much I'd charge (or what I'd ask to start with)."

Example: Local café's social media problem

"I've noticed your Instagram posts are sporadic and your stories get low engagement. This probably costs you customers who don't know what you're serving or forget you exist. I can:

  • Create a 4-week content calendar (coffee shots, customer features, daily specials)
  • Post 3x a week consistently
  • Respond to comments and DMs same-day
  • Track what works and optimise

This would probably cost £50-100/week if you hired an agency. I'm offering £40/week and I'll start immediately."

Example: Freelance accountant's booking problem

"Your clients have to email to book appointments, which takes back-and-forth time and sometimes leads to missed bookings. I can set up an online booking system (Calendly) that integrates with your email and sends automatic reminders. You'll probably save 3-5 hours per week. This costs about £1,000 to set up professionally. I can do it for £300 and train you to use it."

The proposal should:

  • Be 1 page maximum
  • Start with the problem you see
  • Explain the cost of that problem (money lost, time wasted)
  • Say exactly what you'll do
  • Give a price or ask what they think is fair
  • Offer to start immediately
  • Be honest about your experience level (if relevant)

Step 4: Actually Pitch (The Scary Part)

Now you have to actually talk to the person in charge.

In-person pitch (best option):

  1. Go to the business during a quiet time (not busy hours)
  2. Ask to speak to the owner/manager: "Hi, I have an idea I think could help your business. Do you have 10 minutes?"
  3. Be respectful of their time. If they're busy, say: "I can come back later—when's a good time?"
  4. Hand them the one-page proposal
  5. Briefly explain what you see and what you're offering
  6. Ask: "Would this be useful?" or "What do you think?"
  7. Listen. Don't oversell.
  8. If they're interested: "When can we start?"
  9. If they're not: "No problem. Could I check back in a month?" (Sometimes they need to think about it.)

Email pitch (if in-person isn't possible):

Email the owner. Subject: "[Business name]: One idea to save you time" or "[Business name]: Quick problem I noticed."

Subject: Local Café: One thing I noticed (& how to fix it)

Hi [Owner name],

I came in yesterday and your coffee is actually brilliant. But I noticed customers are having trouble finding you online or knowing what's fresh that day. That probably costs you new customers.

I could set up simple things to fix this:
- A weekly Instagram schedule (3 posts/week)
- Stories showing daily specials
- Community features (customer photos, events)

I'd do this for £35/week and I can start immediately.

Would this be useful? I can also show you examples of other local businesses I've helped.

Best,
[Your name]
[Your phone]

Step 5: If They Say Yes

Actually deliver. Do better work than you promised. Be responsive. Show results.

This is how you turn one job into a full roster of clients. Do good work on job #1, and job #1 tells their friends.

What If They Say No?

Most will. That's okay. You're learning anyway, and you're building portfolio examples.

Keep a record of every pitch you make. "I pitched 20 businesses. 5 responded. 2 hired me." That's actually impressive.

And some "no"s change to "yes" later. That café owner might email you in 3 months: "Actually, we want to do that Instagram thing." You'll already be in their mind.

The Skills This Teaches You (Beyond Getting Hired)

  • Sales: How to identify problems and pitch solutions. This is the most valuable skill you can learn at 18.
  • Business thinking: You're learning to see things from an owner's perspective. That's everything.
  • Confidence: Walking into a business and pitching an idea is terrifying. Doing it 20 times is empowering.
  • Problem-solving: You have to actually think about what a business needs, not just apply for posted jobs.

Real Talk: Why This Actually Works

Business owners are drowning. They're doing 10 jobs and wish they had help. Someone who walks in with a specific solution to a specific problem they feel is gold. Most never get that. You're offering them something they didn't even know they could have.

You're not competing with 200 applicants. You're the only person offering them this solution. That's an insane advantage.

What You Can Charge (Realistically)

  • Social media management: £30-50/week (£2-6/hour of your time if you're efficient)
  • Setting up systems: £200-500 one-time project
  • Data entry/organisation: £12-18/hour
  • Admin support: £15-20/hour part-time
  • Website updates: £300-1,000 depending on complexity

Start low. Build portfolio examples. Raise rates once you have proof you deliver.

The Bottom Line

Job applications are for people waiting to be chosen. Reverse pitches are for people who choose themselves. You're not asking for a chance. You're offering a solution. That's completely different.

Go pitch 20 businesses. Get hired by 2-3 of them. In 6 months you'll have real clients, portfolio examples, and money. Plus, you'll know more about business than most people learn in university.
