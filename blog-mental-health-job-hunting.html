The Mental Health Toll of Job Hunting at 18

Nobody warns you about this. You send 50 applications and hear back from none. You start believing there's something wrong with you. You refresh your email 100 times a day. You spiral. Here's how to job hunt without destroying your mental health.

Why Job Hunting at 18 is Uniquely Brutal

At 18, your first "real" job application feels like the first impression that matters. Your entire future hangs on it. Or at least it feels that way. So you:

  • Write the "perfect" CV (which takes 10 hours)
  • Customise every cover letter (which takes hours more)
  • Obsess over every word
  • Send it off and wait
  • Refresh your email compulsively
  • Get rejected (or get ghosted)
  • Feel like you've failed
  • Repeat with the next job, but feel worse each time

By application #30, you're convinced you're unemployable. By application #50, you're considering giving up entirely.

What's Actually Happening (The Psychology)

Rejection sensitivity: You're more sensitive to rejection at 18 because your identity is still forming. A job rejection feels like you are being rejected, not your application.

Perfectionism trap: You believe if you're perfect, you'll get the job. So you work harder and harder on the same thing, when actually you should be applying to more jobs instead.

Feedback void: Most rejections come with zero feedback. You don't know what you did wrong. So you assume the worst about yourself.

Invisible comparisons: Everyone on social media got their job immediately. Everyone got offers. You're the only one stuck. (You're not. Everyone feels this way.)

Scarcity mindset: If you don't get this job, everything is ruined. So you obsess over it instead of moving on.

The Physical Toll (It's Real)

Job hunting stress does actual things to your body:

  • Sleep gets worse. You lie awake thinking about rejection.
  • Appetite changes (or you stress-eat)
  • Your immune system tanks. You get sick more often.
  • Anxiety spikes. You're jumpy. Your chest feels tight.
  • Motivation craters. Even things you normally enjoy feel pointless.
  • Social withdrawal. You avoid friends because you're ashamed about being unemployed.

This isn't weakness. This is your nervous system responding to perceived threat. Your brain thinks: "If I don't get this job, I'll be homeless. So I need to panic constantly." It's not logical, but it's real.

How to Protect Your Mental Health While Job Hunting

1. Reject Perfectionism (This is the Big One)

Your CV doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be good enough and sent to 100 places. A perfect CV sent to 5 jobs gets you nowhere. An 80% CV sent to 50 jobs gets you hired.

New rule: Spend 15 minutes on your CV. Create a template. Use it for every job. Done.

Cover letters: Skip them if the job doesn't require one. If it does, use a template with one personalisable paragraph. Not a custom essay.

Why this works: You're applying to more jobs, so rejection stings less (it's a numbers game, not a reflection of you). You're also less emotionally invested in each application (you didn't spend 3 hours on it).

2. Set Boundaries on Job Hunting Time

Decide: I will job hunt for 2 hours, 3 days a week. That's it. Outside those times, you don't think about it.

During those 2 hours: Apply to jobs. Don't obsess. Don't edit. Don't research companies deeply. Apply and move on.

Outside those hours: Do literally anything else. Hobby. Sport. Social. Sleep. Not job hunting.

Why this works: Your brain needs off-ramps. If you job hunt 24/7, you'll burn out in 2 weeks. Structured limits actually make you more productive.

3. Uninstall Email From Your Phone (Seriously)

You're refreshing your email 50 times a day. Each refresh is a small dopamine hit followed by crushing disappointment. You're training yourself to be anxious.

Delete the email app. Check email once a day on a computer, at a set time.

Why this works: You'll get the email either way. But checking obsessively does nothing except make you anxious. Once-a-day checking is enough.

4. Track Numbers, Not Feelings

Instead of obsessing over rejections, track:

  • Number of applications sent (goal: 10/week minimum)
  • Number of interviews (goal: 1-2/week after sending 10+ applications)
  • Number of offers (goal: eventually 1)

Don't track: How good your CV is. How fair the rejection was. Whether they liked you. None of that matters. The only thing that matters is: did you apply?

Why this works: Numbers are neutral. Your feelings aren't. A metric like "I applied to 15 jobs this week" is factual and actionable. "I feel like a failure" is not helpful.

5. Reframe Rejection (It's Not About You)

When you get rejected, the real reasons are:

  • They hired someone with more specific experience (not because you're bad)
  • They had an internal candidate (nothing to do with you)
  • The job got cancelled (not your fault)
  • They ghosted because their systems are chaotic (says nothing about you)
  • Someone else was slightly better fit (doesn't mean you're not good)

What rejection does NOT mean:

  • You're not good enough
  • You'll never get a job
  • Something is fundamentally wrong with you
  • You wasted your time

New mantra: "Rejection is about their needs, not my worth."

Why this works: It's true. And it stops you from internalising every rejection as a personal failure.

6. Talk to People (Not About the Job Hunt)

Don't spend time talking about job hunting. Spend time with people, doing things that aren't about jobs.

Why: If you're constantly talking about how you can't find a job, you're:

  • Reinforcing the narrative that you're failing
  • Boring your friends
  • Making yourself feel worse

Instead, maintain your identity outside of job status. Do hobbies. Hang with friends. Live your life. The job is one part of your existence, not the whole thing.

7. Move Your Body Every Day

I know this sounds obvious. But it's the #1 thing that actually works for job hunting anxiety.

20 minutes of walking. That's it. Not a workout. Just movement. Reduces anxiety significantly.

Why: Your nervous system is stuck in "threat mode." Movement signals safety. Your body calms down.

8. Accept That Some of This is Luck

You can't control whether they hire you. You can control whether you apply. Do your part and accept the rest.

Stoicism 101: Control what you control. Accept what you don't. Your application was good? Sent it. They didn't respond? Not your fault. Move on.

When Job Hunting Becomes Depression

If you're noticing:

  • You haven't left your house in days
  • You're sleeping 14+ hours or not sleeping at all
  • You're not eating (or only stress eating)
  • You're having thoughts of harming yourself
  • You feel hopeless, not just discouraged
  • You can't focus on anything

That's depression, not just job hunt stress. Talk to someone.

  • School counsellor
  • GP
  • Therapist
  • Crisis line (Shout: text SHOUT to 85258)

Job hunting can trigger depression, especially if you have a history of it. This is medical. Get help.

What Actually Gets You Hired

Just so you know: persistence beats perfection. Someone who applies to 100 jobs with an okay CV gets hired. Someone who spends 3 weeks perfecting a CV and sends it to 3 places doesn't.

The people who get jobs aren't smarter or better. They just apply more and don't give up when rejected.

The Reality Check

You will get a job. It might not be the one you wanted. It might not be perfect. But you'll get one. Most people do. Not because they're amazing. Because they kept applying.

Your first job is not your last job. It's not even important, really. It's just a stepping stone. You'll be fine.

Job Hunt Mental Health Checklist

I've set a time limit for job hunting (max 2-3 hours, 3x/week)
I've deleted email from my phone
I'm tracking applications sent (not feelings)
I'm doing something physical most days
I have friends I talk to about non-job-hunting stuff
I'm sleeping (7-9 hours a night)
I know I can reach out for help if this becomes depression

You're Not Alone in This

Every young person job hunting feels this way. The anxiety, the rejection, the self-doubt. It's normal. But you don't have to handle it alone. SAFE is here if you need support.
