The "Runaway" Reality: Emergency Housing & Your Legal Rights

You're thinking about leaving. You don't know what comes next. This is what actually happens when you leave home: emergency housing, police interactions, your legal rights. Real information. No judgement.

Before You Leave: What You Actually Need to Know

First: leaving home because you're unsafe is different from leaving because you're angry. This guide is for the first one. If you're in danger, you need to leave. If you're angry about rules, talk to a counsellor first.

Second: you have more rights and support than you think. You're not alone in this. The system isn't perfect, but it exists.

The First 24 Hours (Immediate Safety)

If You're Under 16

You have emergency shelter rights. Use them.

What to do:

  1. Call your local council emergency duty team (search "[your council] emergency duty" or dial 0800 number for your area)
  2. Say: "I'm under 16 and I'm not safe at home. I need emergency accommodation."
  3. They'll assess you. This usually takes a few hours.
  4. If they agree you're unsafe, they'll place you somewhere: foster care, a hostel, a children's home, or supported lodgings.
  5. A social worker will be assigned to you.

What to bring (if you can):

  • ID or birth certificate (if you have it)
  • Your National Insurance number
  • Medical information
  • Anything that proves abuse (photos, messages, documentation)
  • Phone charger
  • Medications
  • A comfort item

What happens next: You'll sleep somewhere safe that night. Tomorrow, a social worker will talk to you. You'll make a safety plan.

If You're 16-17

Same as above, but you might get more independence. You could be placed in supported lodgings (an adult's home with support) rather than a children's home. You'll have more say in what happens next.

If You're 18+

This is harder. You're legally an adult.

If you were "looked after" before 18: You get "leaving care" services. They'll help with housing, money, support. Call them first.

If you weren't: Contact homeless services directly. Search "[your council] homeless emergency accommodation." The system is less supportive, but housing exists.

Important: Don't wait until you're homeless to act. Contact services before you're on the street. It's much easier to get support if you reach out early.

Dealing With Police (What You Need to Know)

If Police Stop You

You can be stopped and asked questions. But you have rights.

Your rights:

  • Right to silence: You don't have to answer questions. Say: "I want to speak to a solicitor" and stop talking.
  • Right to a solicitor: Police must provide one before questioning (free if you can't afford it).
  • Right to a responsible adult: If you're under 18, an adult (not a police officer) must be present.
  • Right to know why: They must tell you why they stopped you.
  • Right to refuse searches: They can't search you without permission (unless they have a warrant or you're in immediate danger).

What to say: "I'm under 18. I want to speak to a solicitor. I'm not answering questions without a solicitor and a responsible adult."

Repeat this if they pressure you. Don't answer questions. Police will try to get you to talk. Your silence is your power.

If Police Bring You Home

Sometimes police stop you, find out you're under 18, and bring you home. This isn't great, but it's not the worst outcome.

What happens: Police bring you home. They might talk to your parents. They might note it as a "runaway" report.

What's important: If you told police you're unsafe, they should inform social services. If they don't, you can call social services yourself and report it.

If You're Arrested

Being homeless or "running away" isn't a crime. You can't be arrested for that. But you might be arrested if police think you've committed a crime (theft to survive, for example).

If arrested:

  1. You have a right to a solicitor. Ask for one immediately.
  2. Don't answer questions without a solicitor present.
  3. Tell them your age. Tell them you're unsafe at home.
  4. Tell them you need social services involvement.

A good solicitor will fight for you. A social worker can support your case. You're not alone here.

Dealing With Children's Social Care (Social Services)

Quick note on terms: In the UK, this is "Children's Social Care" or "social services" — not the American "CPS." (Confusingly, the UK has a CPS too — the Crown Prosecution Service — but that's a separate body that prosecutes crime.) The people you'll meet are social workers from your local council.

If They Contact Your Home

A social worker might show up at your house if someone reports abuse or neglect. This is scary, but it's also protection.

Your rights:

  • You can be interviewed alone (without your parents)
  • You can have a trusted adult present during interviews
  • You can ask for a solicitor
  • You don't have to answer questions alone

What to do: Be honest. Tell them what's happening. They're not trying to punish you or your parents. They're trying to assess safety.

If They Remove You

Children's Social Care can remove you if you're in danger. This is sudden and scary.

What happens:

  1. Social workers and police show up and remove you (emergency intervention)
  2. You go to emergency accommodation (foster care, children's home)
  3. A court hearing happens within days
  4. A judge decides if you stay removed or go back home

This sucks, but it's temporary. Even if you're removed, you can work toward going back home (if it becomes safe) or toward independence (if it doesn't).

Emergency Housing: What It Actually Is

For Under 16s

Foster care: You live with a family. They get paid to care for you. Many foster carers are kind. Some aren't great. It's a lottery. But you're safe and fed.

Children's home: You live with other young people in a supervised house. Staff are there 24/7. Less homey, but structured and safe.

Supported lodgings: You live with an adult who's trained to support young people. More independence than foster care, less than a children's home.

For 16-17s

Same options, but you might be offered more choice about where you go.

For 18+

Hostels: Shared rooms, basic facilities, support workers on-site

Supported accommodation: Your own room/flat, but support services check in regularly

Shared housing: House-share with other young people leaving care, supported by agency

The Emotional Reality (Not Just the Practical Stuff)

Leaving home, even when you need to, is traumatic. You're grieving. You're angry. You might feel relief. You might feel guilt. All of this is normal.

You might:

  • Feel homesick even though home was unsafe
  • Blame yourself for leaving
  • Want to go back (even though you know it's not safe)
  • Feel angry at systems that "took you away"
  • Feel lonely in emergency housing
  • Struggle with the fact that your family is still there, without you

This is normal. You need a counsellor/therapist. Ask your social worker for one. They'll provide it for free.

What to Do Right Now (If You're Thinking About Leaving)

  1. Talk to someone: school counsellor, trusted teacher, friend's parent, or call a crisis line
  2. Document abuse (if it's safe to do): dates, what happened, any injuries
  3. Know your local council's emergency number: [search "[your council] emergency housing"]
  4. Know these numbers:
    • Childline: 0800 1111
    • Samaritans: 116 123
    • Shelter: 0808 800 4444
  5. Keep your ID/important documents somewhere accessible
  6. Tell at least one trusted adult you're unsafe

The Long View

Leaving home is not a failure. It's survival. Some people need to leave to stay alive or healthy. That's not weakness. That's strength.

What comes after leaving is rebuilding. New housing. New support system. New life. It's hard, but it's possible. Thousands of young people do it.

The system isn't perfect. But it exists. And you deserve to be safe.
